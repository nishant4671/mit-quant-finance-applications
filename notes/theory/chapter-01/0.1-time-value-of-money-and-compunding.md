# 1. What is Time Value of Money (TVM)?

## Definition

The Time Value of Money (TVM) is the financial principle that money available today is worth more than the same amount received in the future because today's money can be invested to earn returns.

Simply put:

Money has earning power.

A ₹1000 note today is not just ₹1000.

It is:

₹1000 + all the future interest it can generate.

Therefore,

Present Money > Future Money

---

# Why does money have time value?

There are several reasons.

## 1. Investment Opportunity

Money today can be invested.

Example

Suppose you receive

₹1000 today.

You invest it at 10%.

After one year

You have

₹1100.

If instead someone gives you ₹1000 after one year,

you lose the opportunity to earn that ₹100.

---

## 2. Inflation

Inflation reduces purchasing power.

Example

Today

₹100 buys

10 chocolates.

After one year

Due to inflation,

₹100 buys only

8 chocolates.

The money is the same.

Its purchasing power is lower.

---

## 3. Risk

Future payments are uncertain.

Questions arise such as:

- What if the borrower defaults?
- What if the company goes bankrupt?
- What if the payment is delayed?

Money received today has no such uncertainty.

---

## 4. Liquidity

Money today can be used immediately.

Future money cannot.

Today's money gives flexibility.

---

# Core Principle

Always remember:

Current Money

↓

Investment

↓

Future Money

This is called

Compounding.

Future Money

↓

Discounting

↓

Current Money

This is called

Discounting.

Compounding and Discounting are opposite operations.

# 2. Present Value (PV)

## Definition

Present Value is the value today of money that will be received in the future.

In other words,

Present Value answers:

"If I will receive money later, what is it worth today?"

---

## Example

Suppose

You will receive

₹110

after one year.

Interest rate

10%

Question:

How much is that worth today?

Answer

₹100

Because

₹100 invested today at 10%

becomes

₹110 after one year.

Therefore

Present Value = ₹100.

---

## Financial Meaning

Present Value tells us

how much we should be willing to pay today for future cash.

---

# 3. Future Value (FV)

## Definition

Future Value is the amount that today's money grows into after earning interest.

Question answered:

"If I invest today, how much money will I have later?"

---

Example

Invest

₹1000

Interest

8%

One year

Future Value

₹1080

---

Financial Meaning

Future Value tells us

how much our investment becomes after compounding.

---

# Relationship Between PV and FV

Present Value

↓

Compounding

↓

Future Value

Future Value

↓

Discounting

↓

Present Value

These are inverse operations.

# 5. Simple Interest

Definition

Interest is calculated only on the original principal.

Formula

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Characteristics

- Interest remains constant every period.
- Previous interest never earns additional interest.

Example

Principal

₹1000

Interest

10%

3 years

Year 1

₹100

Year 2

₹100

Year 3

₹100

Total Interest

₹300

Final Amount

₹1300

# 6. Compound Interest

Definition

Interest is earned on:

- Original principal
- Previous interest

This is called

Interest on Interest.

---

Example

Principal

₹1000

Interest

10%

Year 1

₹1100

Year 2

₹1210

Year 3

₹1331

Notice

The increase each year becomes larger.

Why?

Because interest itself earns interest.

---

Simple Interest

1000

↓

1100

↓

1200

↓

1300

Compound Interest

1000

↓

1100

↓

1210

↓

1331

Compound interest always grows faster than simple interest (for positive interest rates).

# 7. Discrete Compounding

Definition

Interest is added at specific intervals.

Examples

- Yearly
- Half-yearly
- Quarterly
- Monthly
- Weekly
- Daily

Each interval is called a compounding period.

---

Examples

Compounded:

Yearly

Interest added once each year.

Monthly

Interest added 12 times.

Daily

Interest added 365 times.

---

Key Idea

The more frequently interest is compounded,

the larger the Future Value becomes.

---

Example

Principal

₹1000

Interest

10%

One year

Yearly

≈ ₹1100

Monthly

≈ ₹1104.71

Daily

≈ ₹1105.16

Notice

More frequent compounding

↓

More interest earned.

---

# 8. Continuous Compounding

Definition

Instead of adding interest every month or every day,

interest is assumed to be added continuously,

at every infinitesimally small instant.

There are no discrete compounding periods.

Growth occurs continuously.

---

Visualization

Yearly Compounding

Money grows in jumps.

1000

↓

1100

↓

1210

↓

1331

Continuous Compounding

Money grows smoothly like a flowing curve.

---

Why is Continuous Compounding Used?

Continuous compounding is mathematically elegant and is widely used in:

- Quantitative Finance
- Option Pricing (Black-Scholes Model)
- Bond Pricing
- Fixed Income Analytics
- Stochastic Calculus
- Mathematical Finance

Although banks usually compound monthly or daily,

financial models often assume continuous compounding.

---

# 9. Euler's Number (e)

Continuous compounding introduces the mathematical constant

e

Approximately

e ≈ 2.718281828

This number naturally appears whenever growth is continuous.

Examples in mathematics

- Population Growth
- Radioactive Decay
- Compound Interest
- Machine Learning
- Differential Equations
- Finance

# 10. Continuous Compounding Formula

Future Value

FV = PV × e^(rt)

where

PV = Present Value

FV = Future Value

r = Annual interest rate (decimal)

t = Time (years)

e = Euler's Number

---

Interpretation

Current Money

↓

Continuous Growth

↓

Future Money

---

# 11. Continuous Discounting Formula

Present Value

PV = FV × e^(-rt)

This is the reverse of continuous compounding.

Instead of moving money into the future,

we bring future money back to today.

---

Interpretation

Future Money

↓

Continuous Discounting

↓

Present Money

---

# Why is the exponent negative?

Because discounting moves backward through time.

Compounding moves forward.

Forward

+

Backward

−

Positive exponent

Future Value

Negative exponent

Present Value

---

# Example

Suppose

Future Value

₹1000

Interest

5%

Time

2 years

Present Value

PV = 1000 × e^(-0.05×2)

≈ ₹904.84

Meaning

Receiving ₹1000 two years from now is financially equivalent to receiving approximately ₹904.84 today, assuming a continuously compounded 5% annual rate.

---

# 12. Discounting

Definition

Discounting converts future cash flows into today's equivalent value.

Question answered

"What is future money worth today?"

Applications

- Bond Pricing
- Stock Valuation
- Real Estate
- Corporate Finance
- Capital Budgeting

---

# Compounding vs Discounting

Compounding

Today

↓

Future

Money grows.

Discounting

Future

↓

Today

Money shrinks to its present value.

Opposite mathematical operations.

# 15. Common Mistakes

❌ Confusing Present Value with Future Value.

PV is today's value.

FV is tomorrow's value.

---

❌ Thinking simple interest and compound interest are identical.

Simple interest grows linearly.

Compound interest grows exponentially.

---

❌ Forgetting to convert percentages into decimals.

5%

must become

0.05

---

❌ Forgetting the negative sign in

PV = FV × e^(-rt)

Negative exponent indicates discounting.

# 16. Formula Summary

Simple Interest

Interest = Principal × Rate × Time

Compound Interest (Discrete)

FV = PV × (1 + r/n)^(nt)

Continuous Compounding

FV = PV × e^(rt)

Continuous Discounting

PV = FV × e^(-rt)

# 18. Key Exam Points

✔ Money today is worth more than money tomorrow.

✔ Present Value converts future money into today's money.

✔ Future Value converts today's money into future money.

✔ Compound interest earns interest on previous interest.

✔ Continuous compounding assumes growth occurs at every instant.

✔ Euler's number (e) appears naturally in continuous growth.

✔ Continuous discounting uses a negative exponent because money is moved backward through time.

✔ Compounding and discounting are inverse operations.

# 19. One-Sentence Revision

Time Value of Money states that money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future because it can earn returns; compounding grows money forward through time, while discounting brings future money back to its present value, with continuous compounding using Euler's number (e) to model growth occurring at every instant.
