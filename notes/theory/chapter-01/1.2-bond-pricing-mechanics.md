> "A bond is nothing more than a series of future cash flows. Its value today is simply the present value of those future cash flows."

This is one of the most important principles in finance.

Whether the bond is issued by:

- A Government
- A Corporation
- A Municipality
- A Bank

the pricing principle never changes.

Everything reduces to one idea:

> **Price = Present Value of Future Cash Flows**

# 1. What is a Bond?

## Definition

A bond is a financial instrument representing a loan made by an investor to a borrower.

The borrower promises to:

- Pay periodic interest (called coupons, if applicable)
- Repay the principal (face value) at maturity

A bond is essentially an **IOU (I Owe You)**.

---

## Parties Involved

### Issuer

The person or institution borrowing money.

Examples

- Government
- Company
- Municipality

---

### Investor

The person buying the bond and lending money.

Examples

- Individuals
- Banks
- Mutual Funds
- Pension Funds
- Insurance Companies

---

# 5. Bond Pricing Principle

This is the single most important rule.

A bond's value today equals

the present value of every future cash flow.

No exceptions.

Every payment is discounted back to today.

---

Financial Interpretation

Future cash is worth less than present cash.

Therefore

future coupons

and

future principal

must be discounted.

---

# Bond Pricing Formula

Bond Price

=

Present Value of Coupons

+

Present Value of Principal

This principle is used worldwide by:

- Banks
- Central Banks
- Investment Funds
- Quantitative Traders
- Financial Analysts

# 10. Yield to Maturity (YTM)

Definition

Yield to Maturity (YTM) is the single annual interest rate that makes the present value of all future bond cash flows equal to the bond's current market price.

Think of YTM as:

> **The annual return an investor is expected to earn if they buy the bond today, hold it until maturity, receive every coupon, and all payments occur as promised.**

---

Important Assumptions

YTM assumes

✔ Bond is held until maturity.

✔ Coupons are received on time.

✔ Coupons can be reinvested at the same YTM.

✔ No default occurs.

---

# Why Is YTM Called an "Average" Rate?

Real bond cash flows occur at different times.

Ideally,

each payment should have its own discount rate.

YTM simplifies everything.

Instead of using many rates,

it uses

ONE

single discount rate.

That is why YTM is sometimes called

an equivalent average annual yield.

---

# 12. Why Can't We Solve YTM Directly?

For a coupon bond,

the YTM appears inside the discounting term for every cash flow.

The equation has no simple algebraic solution.

Instead,

we must guess a value,

check the resulting bond price,

and improve the guess repeatedly until the calculated price matches the market price.

This is called **numerical root-finding**.

---

# 13. Numerical Root-Finding

Definition

Numerical root-finding is a computational technique used to solve equations that cannot be rearranged into a simple closed-form solution.

In bond pricing,

the unknown variable is the YTM.

The algorithm repeatedly adjusts the interest rate until:

Calculated Bond Price

=

Observed Market Price

---

# Newton-Raphson Method

One of the fastest numerical methods.

General Steps

1. Guess an initial YTM.
2. Calculate the bond price using that guess.
3. Measure the error between calculated price and market price.
4. Update the guess.
5. Repeat until the error is extremely small.

Python libraries such as `scipy.optimize` automate this process.

---

# 14. Why Is YTM Useful?

YTM allows investors to compare bonds with:

- Different prices
- Different maturities

Instead of comparing dozens of cash flows,

# 16. Common Mistakes

❌ Confusing coupon with YTM.

Coupon = fixed payment.

YTM = market-implied return.

---

❌ Thinking bond prices remain constant.

Bond prices change every trading day.

Coupons generally do not.

---

❌ Believing YTM is always equal to the coupon rate.

Only true when:

Bond Price = Face Value.

---

❌ Forgetting that YTM is an estimate based on assumptions.

Actual realized returns may differ if the bond is sold early or coupons are reinvested at different rates.
